Callisto

The second-largest moon of Jupiter and the third-largest moon in the Solar System, believed to harbor a subsurface liquid ocean beneath its icy crust.
Callisto is considered one of the most promising targets for astrobiology research due to its subsurface ocean, which may contain more water than all of Earth's oceans combined. The moon's relatively stable environment and distance from Jupiter's intense radiation make it an attractive target for future exploration missions. Unlike Europa, Callisto's ocean is likely separated from its rocky core by a layer of ice, but it still represents a potentially habitable environment.
Wikipedia NASA Solar System

Cambrian Explosion

A period of rapid evolutionary diversification that occurred approximately 541 million years ago, during which most major animal phyla appeared in the fossil record.
The Cambrian Explosion represents one of the most significant events in Earth's biological history and is considered crucial to the development of complex life. This event is often cited in discussions of the Rare Earth Hypothesis as an example of the unlikely circumstances that led to complex life on Earth. During this period, organisms developed hard shells, complex eyes, and sophisticated body plans that form the basis of modern animal diversity.
Wikipedia

Carbon Dioxide

A colorless, odorless gas (CO₂) that makes up approximately 0.04% of Earth's atmosphere and plays a crucial role in the greenhouse effect and global climate regulation.
Carbon dioxide is a key greenhouse gas that traps heat in Earth's atmosphere through the greenhouse effect. While it represents a small fraction of the atmosphere by volume, its concentration has increased by over 40% since pre-industrial times due to human activities, primarily fossil fuel combustion and deforestation. CO₂ is essential for photosynthesis in plants and is part of the natural carbon cycle, but elevated levels contribute to global warming and climate change. The gas is also found in the atmosphere of Venus, where it creates extreme greenhouse effects. Atmospheric CO₂ levels are measured at observatories like Mauna Loa, Hawaii, and serve as a key indicator of climate change.
Wikipedia

Cardinal West

One of the four main compass directions, pointing toward the direction where the Sun sets during the equinoxes, at 270 degrees on a compass rose.
Cardinal West is one of the four cardinal directions (North, South, East, West) that form the foundation of navigation and orientation systems. West is defined as the direction toward which the Sun appears to set during the spring and fall equinoxes. On a standard compass, west is located at 270 degrees, directly opposite to east (90 degrees). The cardinal directions are fundamental to map reading, navigation, and geographic coordinate systems used worldwide.
Wikipedia

Celsius

A temperature scale where water freezes at 0 degrees and boils at 100 degrees under standard atmospheric pressure, widely used in science and most countries worldwide.
The Celsius scale, originally called centigrade, was developed by Swedish astronomer Anders Celsius in 1742. It is part of the metric system and is the standard temperature scale used in scientific research and weather reporting in most countries. The scale is based on the freezing and boiling points of water, making it intuitive for everyday use. In atmospheric science, Celsius is used to measure air temperature, dew point, and other thermal properties. The scale relates to the Kelvin scale (the SI base unit for temperature) by the simple conversion: °K = °C + 273.15.
Wikipedia

Centigrade

The former name for the Celsius temperature scale, derived from Latin meaning "hundred steps," referring to the 100-degree interval between the freezing and boiling points of water.
The term "centigrade" was used for the temperature scale from its creation in 1742 until 1948, when it was officially renamed "Celsius" to honor Anders Celsius and avoid confusion with the angular measurement also called centigrade. The name centigrade literally means "hundred grades" or "hundred steps," reflecting the 100-degree span between water's freezing and boiling points. While the official name is now Celsius, the term centigrade is still occasionally used, particularly in older scientific literature and some informal contexts.
Wikipedia

CGS

The centimeter-gram-second system of units, a variant of the metric system based on the centimeter as the unit of length, the gram as the unit of mass, and the second as the unit of time.
The CGS system was widely used in physics and chemistry before being largely replaced by the MKS (meter-kilogram-second) system and later the SI system. In the CGS system, force is measured in dynes, energy in ergs, and power in ergs per second. While less common today, CGS units are still used in some specialized fields, particularly in theoretical physics and chemistry, where the smaller base units can be more convenient for calculations involving atomic and molecular scales.
Wikipedia

Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar

Indian-American astrophysicist (1910-1995) who made fundamental contributions to our understanding of stellar structure and evolution, particularly the physics of white dwarf stars.
Chandrasekhar's most famous discovery was the theoretical limit for the mass of white dwarf stars, now known as the Chandrasekhar limit (approximately 1.44 solar masses). He showed that white dwarfs above this mass cannot be supported by electron degeneracy pressure and will collapse, leading to either neutron stars or black holes. His work laid the foundation for understanding stellar evolution and the final stages of stellar life. Chandrasekhar was awarded the 1983 Nobel Prize in Physics, shared with William Fowler, for his theoretical studies of the physical processes important to the structure and evolution of stars. The Chandra X-ray Observatory is named in his honor.
Wikipedia Nobel Prize

Chandrasekhar Limit

The maximum mass (approximately 1.44 solar masses) that a white dwarf star can have before it collapses under its own gravity, leading to a Type Ia supernova explosion.
Named after Nobel Prize-winning astrophysicist Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar, this limit represents a fundamental threshold in stellar evolution. When a white dwarf accretes matter from a companion star and exceeds this mass limit, electron degeneracy pressure can no longer support the star against gravitational collapse. The resulting thermonuclear explosion completely destroys the white dwarf, creating a Type Ia supernova that serves as a "standard candle" for measuring cosmic distances and studying the expansion of the universe.
Wikipedia

Climate Change

Long-term shifts in global or regional climate patterns, primarily attributed to increased levels of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere due to human activities since the mid-20th century.
Climate change refers to significant changes in Earth's climate system over extended periods, with current changes largely driven by human activities that increase atmospheric concentrations of greenhouse gases like carbon dioxide, methane, and nitrous oxide. These gases trap heat in the atmosphere, leading to global warming and associated effects including rising sea levels, changing precipitation patterns, more frequent extreme weather events, and shifts in ecosystems. Climate change is studied through multiple lines of evidence including temperature records, ice core data, satellite observations, and climate models. Understanding atmospheric processes is crucial for predicting future climate scenarios and developing mitigation strategies.
Wikipedia

Clouds

Visible collections of tiny water droplets or ice crystals suspended in the atmosphere, formed when water vapor condenses around microscopic particles called condensation nuclei.
Clouds play a crucial role in Earth's weather and climate system, affecting both incoming solar radiation and outgoing terrestrial radiation. They form through various processes including convection, orographic lifting, and frontal systems. Clouds are classified by altitude (high, middle, low) and appearance (cumulus, stratus, cirrus), with each type indicating different atmospheric conditions. In climate science, clouds represent one of the most complex feedback mechanisms - they can both cool the planet by reflecting sunlight and warm it by trapping heat. Cloud formation requires water vapor, condensation nuclei (tiny particles), and cooling mechanisms that bring air to saturation.
Wikipedia

Condensation

The process by which water vapor in the atmosphere changes from a gas to a liquid state, typically occurring when air becomes saturated and cools to its dew point temperature.
Condensation is a fundamental process in the water cycle and atmospheric science, responsible for cloud formation, precipitation, and dew formation. It occurs when air containing water vapor is cooled to its saturation point, either through radiative cooling, adiabatic expansion during rising motion, or contact with cooler surfaces. The process requires condensation nuclei - tiny particles such as dust, sand, or bacteria - around which water droplets can form. Condensation releases latent heat, which can fuel further atmospheric processes like thunderstorm development. Understanding condensation is essential for weather prediction and climate modeling.
Wikipedia

Confidence Interval

A range of values where the endpoints are included, representing the range within which the true value of a measurement lies with a specified probability.
When we write a scientific measurement as 12.34 ± 0.02 cm, following the instructions produces a confidence interval: (12.32, 12.36). This interval represents a range where we can assign a probability - like 67% or 100% - that the confidence interval "captures" the true value of the measurement. All scientific measurements are fundamentally probability statements, acknowledging that no measurement can be written with absolute certainty.
Wikipedia

Conjunction

An astronomical event when two or more celestial objects appear close together in the sky as viewed from Earth, or when a superior planet passes behind the Sun from Earth's perspective.
Conjunctions occur when celestial objects have similar celestial coordinates as seen from Earth, making them appear close together even though they may be vastly different distances away. Specifically, for the Solar System, a conjunction occurs when a superior planet (like Mars or Jupiter) passes behind the Sun from Earth's perspective, making it temporarily invisible. In contrast, an inferior conjunction happens when an inferior planet (Venus or Mercury) passes between Earth and the Sun, and a superior conjunction occurs when an inferior planet travels behind the Sun from Earth's perspective.
Wikipedia

Convection Cells

Circular patterns of air movement in the atmosphere where warm air rises, cools, and sinks back down, creating organized circulation systems that transport heat and moisture.
Convection cells are fundamental to atmospheric circulation and weather patterns. They form when surface heating creates temperature differences that drive vertical air motion. Warm, less dense air rises while cooler, denser air sinks, creating a circulation loop. These cells occur at various scales, from small thermal updrafts that create cumulus clouds to large-scale Hadley cells that drive global circulation patterns. In the tropics, Hadley cells transport heat from the equator toward the poles, while smaller convection cells are responsible for thunderstorm development and local weather patterns. Understanding convection cells is essential for weather prediction and climate modeling.
Wikipedia

Conserved Physical Quantity

A physical quantity that can neither be created nor destroyed, with its amount remaining fixed within a closed system under observation.
Examples of conserved physical quantities include mass (classically speaking), energy (classically speaking), electric charge, linear momentum (for isolated systems), and angular momentum (for isolated systems). The beauty of conserved quantities is that they provide additional equations to help solve for unknown physical values. According to Noether's theorem, for every symmetry seen in nature, there is a corresponding conserved physical quantity.
Wikipedia

Constellation

Regions or patches of the sky, with 88 constellations in total covering the entire celestial sphere.
Think of the whole sky as a spherical quilt made of 88 patches, each representing a constellation. The constellations that the Sun appears to pass through on its yearly journey are called zodiacal constellations. These patterns in the sky have been used for navigation and timekeeping throughout human history, though the apparent motion of celestial objects through constellations is due to Earth's orbital motion around the Sun.
Wikipedia

Conversion Factors

Mathematical relationships used to convert measurements within one unit system, or from one unit system to another, expressed as ratios that equal unity when properly constructed.
Conversion factors are essential tools in scientific calculations, allowing scientists to work within a measurement system, or between two measurement systems. For example, to convert from inches to centimeters, we use the conversion factor 2.54 cm/1 inch. The key principle is that conversion factors must equal 1 when the numerator and denominator represent the same physical quantity. Common conversion factors include length (1 meter = 39.37 inches), mass (1 kilogram = 2.205 pounds mass), and energy (1 joule = 0.239 calories). Proper use of conversion factors ensures dimensional consistency in calculations.
Wikipedia

Coriolis Effect

An apparent deflection of moving objects (including air masses) caused by Earth's rotation, resulting in clockwise deflection in the Northern Hemisphere and counterclockwise deflection in the Southern Hemisphere.
The Coriolis effect is fundamental to understanding atmospheric circulation, ocean currents, and weather patterns. It causes moving air masses to curve rather than travel in straight lines, leading to the formation of cyclones and anticyclones. In the Northern Hemisphere, low-pressure systems rotate counterclockwise while high-pressure systems rotate clockwise, with the opposite occurring in the Southern Hemisphere. The effect is strongest at the poles and weakest at the equator, where it becomes zero. The Coriolis effect is responsible for the trade winds, westerlies, and polar easterlies that drive global atmospheric circulation patterns. It also influences ocean currents and is crucial for understanding large-scale weather systems like hurricanes and typhoons.
Wikipedia

Cosmic Distance Ladder

A series of methods by which astronomers determine distances to celestial objects, with each method building upon and calibrating the next to reach progressively greater distances.
The cosmic distance ladder begins with direct geometric methods like stellar parallax for nearby stars, then uses standard candles like Cepheid variable stars for intermediate distances, and Type Ia supernovae for the most distant galaxies. Each "rung" of the ladder must be carefully calibrated using the previous rung, making the entire system interdependent. This systematic approach allows astronomers to measure distances across the observable universe, from nearby stars to galaxies billions of light-years away, and is fundamental to our understanding of cosmic expansion and the scale of the universe.
Wikipedia

Cosmic Inflation

A period of extremely rapid exponential expansion of the universe that occurred in the first fraction of a second after the Big Bang, during which the observable universe grew from subatomic to macroscopic size.
Cosmic inflation lasted less than a trillionth of a second but expanded the observable universe by about 26 orders of magnitude - from the size of a proton to the size of a grapefruit. This process explains the remarkable uniformity of the cosmic microwave background. Inflation theory explains several key features of our universe, including its flatness, uniformity, and the origin of the density fluctuations that eventually became galaxies and stars.
Wikipedia

Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB)

The thermal radiation left over from the Big Bang, filling the entire universe and providing a snapshot of the cosmos when it was only 380,000 years old.
The CMB was released during the recombination epoch when the universe cooled enough for electrons and protons to form neutral hydrogen atoms, making the universe transparent to light for the first time. Originally at 3000 Kelvin, this radiation has been redshifted to 2.73 Kelvin today. The CMB provides crucial evidence for the Big Bang theory and allows scientists to study the early universe's conditions and composition with extraordinary precision.
Wikipedia ESA Planck

Cosmological Constant

A term in Einstein's field equations representing the energy density of empty space, originally introduced to allow for a static universe but now associated with dark energy.
Einstein initially added the cosmological constant (Λ) to his equations to prevent the universe from expanding or contracting. After Hubble's discovery of cosmic expansion, Einstein called it his "greatest mistake." However, the discovery of accelerating expansion has revived interest in this concept. The cosmological constant is now the leading explanation for dark energy, the mysterious force driving the universe's accelerating expansion.
Wikipedia

Cosmological Redshift

The stretching of light wavelengths due to the expansion of space itself, causing distant objects to appear redder than they actually are.
As the universe expands, the space between galaxies stretches, and light traveling through this space gets stretched as well. This is different from Doppler redshift, which is caused by motion through space rather than the expansion of space itself. Cosmological redshift is a key tool for measuring distances to far galaxies and studying the expansion history of the universe.
Wikipedia

Cosmology

The evolution of the Universe, past, present and future.
Cosmology is the scientific study of the large-scale properties and evolution of the universe as a whole. It encompasses the origin, evolution, and eventual fate of the universe, including the study of cosmic microwave background, dark matter, dark energy, and the formation of large-scale structures.
Wikipedia

Crossover Point

The moment in cosmic history when dark energy began to dominate over matter in driving the universe's expansion, occurring approximately 5 billion years ago.
Before the crossover point, matter (both ordinary and dark) dominated the universe's dynamics, causing expansion to decelerate. After this point, dark energy became the dominant force, causing the expansion to accelerate. This transition fundamentally changed the universe's fate from potential collapse to eternal expansion.
Wikipedia

e. e. cummings

American poet who wrote "Listen, there's a hell of a universe next door, let's go!"
Edward Estlin Cummings (1894-1962) was an American poet, painter, essayist, author, and playwright known for his innovative use of language and form. His quote about the universe reflects a sense of wonder and adventure about cosmic exploration and the vastness of space.

Cumulonimbus Clouds

Towering, dense clouds that can extend from near the ground to the top of the troposphere, characterized by their anvil-shaped tops and association with thunderstorms, heavy rain, and severe weather.
Cumulonimbus clouds are the most dramatic and powerful cloud type, often called "thunderheads" due to their association with thunderstorms. They form through strong convective processes when warm, moist air rises rapidly through the atmosphere, creating towering structures that can reach heights of 12-18 km (40,000-60,000 feet). The characteristic anvil shape at the top forms when the rising air hits the tropopause and spreads horizontally. These clouds produce lightning, thunder, heavy precipitation, hail, tornadoes, and strong winds. They are classified as vertical development clouds and represent some of the most energetic atmospheric phenomena, with updrafts that can exceed 30 m/s (100 ft/s).
Wikipedia

Curiosity Rover

A NASA Mars rover that landed on Mars in 2012 and has been exploring Gale Crater, providing crucial evidence for past water activity on Mars.
Curiosity has made groundbreaking discoveries including evidence of ancient riverbeds, seasonal variations in methane levels, and organic compounds in Martian rocks. These findings have significantly advanced our understanding of Mars' potential for past or present life. The rover's sophisticated instruments continue to analyze the Martian environment, searching for conditions that could have supported microbial life.
Wikipedia NASA Mars Mission
