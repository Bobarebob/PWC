Baryonic Matter

Ordinary matter composed of protons, neutrons, and electrons - the familiar matter that makes up stars, planets, and living beings.
Baryonic matter represents only about 4.6% of the total universe. The term "baryonic" comes from baryons, a class of particles that includes protons and neutrons. This is the matter we can see and interact with directly. Despite being the foundation of all visible structures in the universe, baryonic matter is vastly outnumbered by dark matter and dark energy, highlighting how little we understand about the universe's true composition.
Wikipedia

Beam Balance Scales

Analog measuring devices commonly used in physical science to measure mass, typically with a least count of 0.1 grams.
Beam balance scales are less precise than micrometers, so they typically use the 100% rule for determining tolerance - meaning the tolerance equals the least count. For a typical beam balance scale with a least count of 0.1 grams, the tolerance is also 0.1 gram, so a measurement might be written as 45.3 ± 0.1 grams. These scales work by comparing the mass of an unknown object to known standard masses.
Wikipedia

Best Guess or Best Estimate

The first numerical part of a scientific measurement, representing the most likely value based on the measurement taken.
In a scientific measurement like 12.34 ± 0.02 cm, the best guess or best estimate is 12.34. This represents our single best estimate of the true value. When making many identical measurements (on the order of 100 measurements), statistics may allow us to produce a better best guess, typically the arithmetic average of all measurements. The best guess is always accompanied by a tolerance and unit to form a complete scientific measurement.
Wikipedia

Big Bang

The prevailing cosmological model describing the universe's expansion from an initial state of high density and temperature approximately 13.8 billion years ago.
The Big Bang theory explains how the universe evolved from a hot, dense initial state to its current form. Evidence includes the cosmic microwave background radiation, the abundance of light elements, and the observed expansion of the universe. The theory was first proposed by Georges Lemaître and later developed by many scientists.
Wikipedia NASA Big Bang

Big Chill

The predicted ultimate fate of the universe, where continued accelerating expansion leads to a cold, dark, and empty cosmos with no remaining free sources of energy.
With dark energy driving accelerated expansion, the universe will continue to expand and cool indefinitely. Stars will burn out, black holes will evaporate through Hawking radiation, and the universe will reach maximum entropy. This scenario, also called the "Heat Death" of the universe, is estimated to occur in about 100 trillion years - more than 7,000 times longer than the current age of the universe.
Wikipedia

Big Crunch

A hypothetical scenario where the universe's expansion would eventually stop and reverse, causing all matter and energy to collapse back into a functional singularity - essentially the Big Bang in reverse.
Before the discovery of dark energy and accelerating expansion, some cosmologists theorized that if the universe contained enough matter, gravity would eventually overcome expansion and cause a contraction phase. The discovery of dark energy in 1998 made the Big Crunch scenario highly unlikely, as the accelerating expansion appears to be permanent.
Wikipedia

Big G

The universal gravitational constant (G), a fundamental physical constant that determines the strength of gravitational attraction between masses.
Big G has a value of approximately 6.674 × 10^-11 m³/kg⋅s². It appears in Newton's law of universal gravitation and Einstein's field equations. The precise value of this constant appears to be fine-tuned to allow the formation of stars, planets, and life. If Big G were significantly different, the universe might not have developed the structures necessary for life to emerge.
Wikipedia

Biomarkers

Chemical signatures or biological indicators that could suggest the presence of past or present life on other planets or moons.
Scientists use space telescopes like the James Webb Space Telescope to analyze the atmospheres of exoplanets, looking for biomarkers such as oxygen, methane, water vapor, or other gases that might indicate biological processes. These signatures help researchers identify potentially habitable worlds and prioritize targets for further study. Biomarkers can be atmospheric gases, surface features, or even technosignatures that indicate the presence of intelligent life. The detection of biomarkers would represent one of the most significant discoveries in human history.
Wikipedia

Blackbody Radiation

The thermal electromagnetic radiation emitted by an object in thermal equilibrium, characterized by a continuous spectrum that depends only on the object's temperature.
All objects with temperature above absolute zero emit blackbody radiation. The cosmic microwave background is an example of blackbody radiation from the early universe, now cooled to 2.73 Kelvin. Understanding blackbody radiation was crucial for the development of quantum mechanics and helps astronomers determine the temperatures of stars and other cosmic objects.
Wikipedia

Giordano Bruno

Italian philosopher, mathematician, and astronomer (1548-1600) who was burned at the stake for his heretical ideas, including the belief in an infinite universe with countless inhabited worlds.
Bruno was one of the earliest advocates of cosmic pluralism, proposing that the universe contained infinite worlds with life. His ideas were considered heretical by the Catholic Church, and he was executed in 1600. He is now recognized as a martyr for scientific thought and free inquiry, and his ideas about multiple worlds have proven remarkably prescient.
Wikipedia Stanford Encyclopedia
