Panspermia

The hypothesis that life exists throughout the universe and is distributed by meteoroids, asteroids, comets, and planetoids, potentially seeding life on Earth from space.
While not widely accepted by the scientific community, panspermia offers an alternative explanation for the origin of life on Earth. The discovery of extremophiles that can survive in space-like conditions has lent some credibility to the possibility of life traveling between worlds. Some scientists propose that life could spread throughout the galaxy via natural processes or even deliberate seeding by advanced civilizations.
Wikipedia

Arno Penzias

American radio astronomer who, along with Robert Wilson, accidentally discovered the cosmic microwave background radiation in 1964, providing crucial evidence for the Big Bang theory.
While working at Bell Telephone Labs on a microwave antenna for telecommunications, Penzias and Wilson detected persistent background "noise" that they couldn't explain. This turned out to be the cosmic microwave background radiation - the afterglow of the Big Bang itself. This discovery provided the first direct observational evidence for the Big Bang theory and earned Penzias and Wilson the 1978 Nobel Prize in Physics.
Wikipedia Nobel Prize

Saul Perlmutter

American astrophysicist and Nobel Prize laureate who led the Supernova Cosmology Project that co-discovered the accelerating expansion of the universe.
Perlmutter's team competed with Brian Schmidt's High-Z Supernova Search Team to measure the universe's expansion rate using Type Ia supernovae. Both teams independently discovered that the expansion is accelerating. Perlmutter shared the 2011 Nobel Prize in Physics for discovering cosmic acceleration, fundamentally changing our understanding of the universe and leading to the concept of dark energy.
Wikipedia Nobel Prize

Phosphine

A chemical compound (PH3) that, when detected in planetary atmospheres, could potentially indicate biological processes, as it is primarily produced by living organisms on Earth.
In 2020, scientists reported the detection of phosphine in Venus's atmosphere, sparking excitement about the possibility of life in the planet's upper clouds. However, this detection remains controversial and requires further verification. On Earth, phosphine is produced by anaerobic bacteria and industrial processes, making it a potential biosignature for life elsewhere.
Wikipedia

Planck Mission

European Space Agency mission (2009-2013) that created the most detailed map of the cosmic microwave background radiation, providing precise measurements of the universe's age, composition, and geometry.
The Planck spacecraft measured temperature variations in the CMB with unprecedented precision, determining that the universe is 13.82 billion years old and consists of 4.9% ordinary matter, 26.8% dark matter, and 68.3% dark energy. Planck's measurements confirmed the standard model of cosmology and provided the most accurate determination of fundamental cosmological parameters to date.
Wikipedia ESA Planck

Proxima Centauri

The closest known star to the Sun, located about 4.24 light-years away in the Alpha Centauri star system, and host to at least one potentially habitable exoplanet.
Proxima Centauri b, discovered in 2016, orbits within the star's habitable zone and has been the target of SETI observations. The system represents the nearest possible destination for interstellar travel and communication attempts. Recent studies suggest the planet may have conditions suitable for liquid water, though it likely faces challenges from stellar radiation.
Wikipedia
