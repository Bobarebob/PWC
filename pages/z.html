Zenial Solar Altitude

The angular height of the Sun above the horizon when it reaches its highest point in the sky (zenith) for a given location and date.
Zenial Solar Altitude varies with latitude and season, reaching its maximum value at solar noon. At the equator during the equinoxes, the zenial solar altitude is 90Â° (directly overhead). At higher latitudes, the maximum solar altitude decreases, and during winter months, the Sun never reaches very high in the sky. This concept is fundamental to understanding solar energy applications, sundial design, and the seasonal variation of daylight hours. The zenial solar altitude is also called the solar elevation angle and is the complement of the solar zenith angle.

Zero (Calibration) Error

A systematic error that occurs when a measuring instrument does not read zero when it should, requiring correction by subtracting/adding the zero reading from/to all subsequent measurements.
Zero calibration error is common in measuring devices like digital calipers, balances, and other precision instruments. For example, if digital calipers read 0.02 mm when its jaws are closed (instead of 0.00 mm), this represents a zero error of +0.02 mm. To correct for this error, you must subtract 0.02 mm from all measurements taken with those calipers (or better, press the tare button, if one is available). Proper scientific practice requires checking for and correcting zero errors before taking measurements. This type of systematic error (a trivial one) affects the accuracy of measurements but not their precision, as it shifts all readings by the same constant amount.

Zodiac

The region of the sky above and below the ecliptic plane through which the planets travel, containing the zodiacal constellations.
The zodiac is the band of sky through which the Sun appears to move during its yearly journey as viewed from Earth. This region contains the constellations that the Sun "passes through" on its apparent annual path. Traditional astrology recognizes 12 zodiacal constellations, but the Sun actually passes through 13 constellations, including Ophiuchus between Scorpius and Sagittarius. Due to precession of Earth's axis over 26,000 years, the actual positions of zodiacal constellations have shifted from their traditional astrological assignments.
Wikipedia

Zodiacal Constellation

The constellations that the Sun appears to pass through on its yearly journey around Earth (in the geocentric model) or that the Sun appears to move through as viewed from Earth (in the heliocentric model).
There are actually 13 zodiacal constellations, not the 12 traditionally recognized by astrology. The 13th constellation is Ophiuchus, located between Scorpius and Sagittarius. These constellations form the backdrop for the Sun's apparent annual motion through the sky, which is actually due to Earth's orbital motion around the Sun. The zodiacal constellations have been used for navigation, timekeeping, and astrological purposes throughout human history.
Wikipedia

Zulu Time

Military and aviation term for Coordinated Universal Time (UTC), named after the NATO phonetic alphabet designation for the letter Z in time zone notation.
Zulu Time is used extensively in aviation, military operations, and international coordination to avoid confusion between different time zones. Since UTC is designated as the Z time zone (UTC+0), it became known as "Zulu time" in radio communications. This standardized time reference ensures clear communication across different geographic locations and time zones. Zulu time does not observe daylight saving time, making it a stable reference for scheduling international operations, flight plans, and scientific observations.
