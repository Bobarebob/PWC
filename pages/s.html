Brian Schmidt

Australian-American astrophysicist and Nobel Prize laureate who co-discovered the accelerating expansion of the universe through observations of distant supernovae.
As leader of the High-Z Supernova Search Team, Schmidt worked alongside Adam Riess and competed with Saul Perlmutter's team to measure the universe's expansion rate using Type Ia supernovae as standard candles. Schmidt shared the 2011 Nobel Prize in Physics for discovering that the universe's expansion is accelerating, fundamentally changing our understanding of cosmology and leading to the concept of dark energy.
Wikipedia Nobel Prize

Scientific Measurement

A quantitative observation composed of three essential parts: a best guess or best estimate, a tolerance or instrument limiting error, and a unit.
Every scientific measurement follows the format: best guess ± tolerance unit. For example, 12.34 ± 0.02 cm represents a complete scientific measurement where 12.34 is the best guess, ± 0.02 is the tolerance, and cm is the unit. Scientific measurements are fundamentally probability statements - we acknowledge that no measurement can be written with absolute certainty, but we can assign a probability that the confidence interval captures the true value of the measurement.
Wikipedia

Scientific Method

A logical and collaborative effort designed to determine the laws and principles of nature, consisting of two parts: the experimental method (doing experiments) and the protocol (advisory principles needed to do science).
The scientific method is based on Aristotle's principle that "knowledge is the product of experience" - we learn about the external world through data acquired through our senses. Experiments are logical procedures that attempt to prove or disprove hypotheses using qualitative and quantitative observations, and must be repeatable. The method requires that all scientific knowledge be verified through experiment, and includes advisory principles like Occam's Principle and the Principle of Falsifiability to guide scientific investigation.
Wikipedia

Scientific Notation

A method of expressing very large or very small numbers in the form a × 10^n, where a is a number equal or greater than 1 and less than 10, and n is an integer exponent.
Scientific notation is essential for handling the extreme scales encountered in science, from subatomic particles to cosmic distances. For example, the speed of light (299,792,458 m/s) can be written as 2.99792458 × 10^8 m/s, while the mass of an electron (0.000000000000000000000000000000911 kg) becomes 9.11 × 10^-31 kg. This notation makes calculations more manageable and clearly shows the number of significant figures in a measurement. Scientific notation is particularly important in physics, chemistry, and astronomy where quantities span many orders of magnitude.
Wikipedia

Scientific Theory

A proven hypothesis that has been conclusively demonstrated through repeated experimentation and observation. In science, a theory is a fact, not a speculation.
When an hypothesis is conclusively proven by experiment, it becomes a scientific theory or fact. This represents the highest level of scientific certainty achievable through the scientific method. Scientific theories of general applicability are called physical laws or physical principles, such as Newton's Law of Gravity or Archimedes' Principle. The term "theory" in science has a much stronger meaning than in everyday language - it represents established scientific knowledge that has been repeatedly verified through experimentation and observation.
Wikipedia

Second Law of Thermodynamics

A fundamental principle of physics stating that the entropy of an isolated system never decreases over time, and that energy spontaneously flows from regions of higher concentration to regions of lower concentration.
This law is crucial in astrobiology for understanding how life maintains order and complexity in a universe trending toward disorder. Living organisms create local decreases in entropy by consuming energy, which is consistent with the Second Law when considering the system as a whole. The law helps explain why life requires a continuous input of energy to maintain its organized state.
Wikipedia

SETI

The Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligence, a scientific effort to detect signs of intelligent life beyond Earth through various methods including radio signal detection and optical searches.
SETI research began in earnest in the 1960s with Frank Drake's Project Ozma and continues today through organizations like the SETI Institute. Modern SETI uses advanced radio telescopes and signal processing techniques to search for artificial signals from extraterrestrial civilizations. The field has expanded to include searches for technosignatures and optical signals.
Wikipedia SETI Institute

Sidereal Moon Day

The time it takes for the Moon to rotate once on its axis relative to the distant stars, which is exactly 27.25 days and identical to the sidereal month.
Because the Moon is tidally locked with Earth, the sidereal Moon day (rotation period) equals the sidereal month (orbital period around Earth). This synchronization means the same side of the Moon always faces Earth. The sidereal Moon day is measured relative to the distant stars, not the Sun, which is why it differs from the synodic month (29.5 days) that we observe as the cycle of lunar phases. This tidal locking occurred over billions of years due to gravitational forces between Earth and the Moon, and it's the reason we only see the near side of the Moon from Earth.
Wikipedia

Sidereal Moon Month

The time it takes for the Moon to complete one orbit around Earth relative to the distant stars, lasting exactly 27.25 days and identical to the sidereal Moon day.
The sidereal month represents the Moon's true orbital period around Earth when measured against the fixed background of distant stars. This is different from the synodic month (29.5 days), which is the time between successive new moons as observed from Earth. The sidereal month is shorter because it doesn't account for Earth's motion around the Sun. During the sidereal month, Earth moves about 27 degrees in its orbit, so the Moon must travel an additional 2+ days to align with the Sun and Earth in the same configuration for the next lunar phase cycle. The equality between the sidereal month and sidereal day demonstrates the Moon's tidal locking with Earth.
Wikipedia

Significant Figures (Significant Digits)

The meaningful digits in a measurement that indicate the precision of that measurement, including all certain digits plus one uncertain (or estimated) digit.
Significant figures reflect the precision limitations of measuring instruments and are crucial for proper scientific calculations. Rules for determining significant figures include: all non-zero digits are significant, zeros between non-zero digits are significant, leading zeros are not significant, and trailing zeros are for sure significant, only if they are expressed when the measurement is in scientific notation. For example, 12.34 has four significant figures, while 0.00123 has three. When performing multiplication and division calculations, the result should be rounded in the normal fashion to match the lesser number of significant digits of the two factors. Significant figures rules ensure that calculated results don't imply greater precision than the original measurements justify. If a calculation involving measurements is intermediate, it's okay to temporarily carry extra nonsignificant digits.
Wikipedia

Sirius

The brightest star in Earth's night sky, located in the constellation Canis Major and forming one vertex of the Winter Triangle asterism.
Sirius, also known as the Dog Star, has an apparent magnitude of -1.46, making it almost twice as bright as the second-brightest star, Canopus. Located about 8.6 light-years from Earth, Sirius is actually a binary star system consisting of a main-sequence star (Sirius A) and a white dwarf companion (Sirius B). Sirius, along with Betelgeuse and Procyon, forms the prominent Winter Triangle asterism that dominates northern winter skies. The star has been important to many cultures throughout history and serves as a key navigation star.
Wikipedia

Sleet (Freezing Rain)

Precipitation that forms as rain but freezes into ice pellets before reaching the ground, or rain that freezes upon contact with cold surfaces, creating hazardous icy conditions.
Sleet occurs when snowflakes fall through a layer of warm air where they melt into raindrops, then pass through a deeper layer of freezing air that refreezes them into small ice pellets before they reach the surface. Freezing rain forms similarly but with a thinner freezing layer, allowing raindrops to remain liquid until they freeze instantly upon contact with cold surfaces like roads, power lines, and trees. Both phenomena create dangerous travel conditions and can cause significant damage to infrastructure. The key difference lies in the thickness of the freezing air layer - thicker layers produce bouncing ice pellets (sleet), while thinner layers produce the smooth, dangerous ice coating of freezing rain.
Wikipedia NOAA Weather Service

Vesto Slipher

American astronomer who first discovered that distant galaxies are redshifted, providing the initial observational evidence for the expansion of the universe before Edwin Hubble's more famous work.
Working at Lowell Observatory in the 1910s and 1920s, Slipher measured the spectra of spiral nebulae (now known to be galaxies) and found that most were redshifted, indicating they were moving away from Earth. Slipher's redshift measurements provided the crucial observational foundation for Hubble's later discovery of the expanding universe and the formulation of Hubble's Law.
Wikipedia Lowell Observatory

Small Magellanic Cloud (SMC)

A dwarf irregular galaxy that orbits the Milky Way along with the Large Magellanic Cloud, located about 200,000 light-years away and visible as a faint patch in the southern sky.
The Small Magellanic Cloud is the smaller companion to the Large Magellanic Cloud, both of which are satellite galaxies of the Milky Way. Like its larger companion, the SMC was named after Portuguese explorer Ferdinand Magellan, who documented it during his voyage in the early 16th century. The SMC contains active star-forming regions and is being gradually disrupted by the Milky Way's gravitational forces, contributing to the Magellanic Stream of gas and stars that trails behind both Magellanic Clouds. The SMC is about one-tenth the mass of the Large Magellanic Cloud and shows signs of significant gravitational interaction with both the LMC and the Milky Way.
Wikipedia

Snow

Precipitation in the form of ice crystals that form in clouds when temperatures are below the freezing point, creating complex hexagonal structures that fall to Earth as snowflakes.
Snow forms when water vapor in clouds condenses directly into ice crystals around microscopic particles like dust or pollen, a process called deposition. The shape and structure of snowflakes depend on temperature and humidity conditions during formation, with no two snowflakes being exactly identical due to the countless variations in atmospheric conditions they encounter while falling. Snow requires temperatures below 32°F (0°C) throughout the entire atmospheric column from cloud to ground to remain frozen. The accumulation of snow plays crucial roles in Earth's water cycle, climate regulation, and provides fresh water resources for billions of people through seasonal snowmelt. Different types of snow crystals form at different temperatures, from thin plates at around 28°F to intricate dendrites at colder temperatures.
Wikipedia NOAA Science

Sol

A Martian solar day, lasting approximately 24 hours and 37 minutes, which is the time it takes for Mars to rotate once on its axis relative to the Sun.
The term "sol" is used by planetary scientists and Mars mission teams to distinguish a Martian day from an Earth day. Since Mars rotates slightly slower than Earth, a sol is about 2.7% longer than an Earth day. This difference becomes significant for mission planning and operations on Mars, where rovers and landers operate on Martian time. For example, the Mars rovers Spirit, Opportunity, Curiosity, and Perseverance all operate on sol-based schedules. Mission teams often adjust their work schedules to match Martian time during critical mission phases. The term helps avoid confusion when coordinating activities between Earth and Mars, where the day-night cycles are slightly out of sync.
Wikipedia

Solar System

The gravitationally bound system consisting of the Sun and the objects that orbit it, including planets, moons, asteroids, comets, and other celestial bodies.
The correct model of the solar system is the heliocentric model proposed by Copernicus, where the Sun is at the center and planets orbit around it. This model replaced the complex geocentric model that required epicycles and deferents to explain planetary motion. In the heliocentric model, planets closer to the Sun orbit faster (Kepler's Third Law), which elegantly explains the apparent retrograde motion of planets as viewed from Earth. The solar system formed approximately 4.6 billion years ago from the gravitational collapse of a giant molecular cloud.
Wikipedia

Solar Wind

A stream of charged particles, primarily protons and electrons, continuously released from the Sun's outermost atmospheric layer, the corona, traveling through the solar system at high speeds.
The solar wind is created by the outward expansion of plasma from the Sun's corona, which reaches temperatures of over 2 million degrees Fahrenheit. These extremely hot conditions give particles enough energy to escape the Sun's gravitational pull, creating a constant flow that extends throughout the solar system. When the solar wind interacts with Earth's magnetosphere, it can cause geomagnetic storms and create the beautiful aurora displays near the polar regions. The solar wind speed varies from about 300 to 800 kilometers per second, and its intensity fluctuates with solar activity cycles. Solar wind also plays a crucial role in shaping planetary atmospheres and can affect satellite communications and power grids on Earth during periods of high solar activity.
Wikipedia NOAA Space Weather

Standard Notation

The conventional way of writing numbers using digits 0-9 without exponents, as opposed to scientific notation which uses powers of 10.
Standard notation is the familiar decimal number system used in everyday mathematics and measurements. For example, the number 1,500 is written in standard notation, while the same number in scientific notation might be written 1.500 × 10^3, if all the trailing zeroes were actually measured. Standard notation is practical for numbers that are not extremely large or small, but becomes unwieldy for very large numbers (like 299,792,458 for the speed of light in m/s) or very small numbers (like 0.000000000000000000000000000000911 for the mass of an electron in kg). In scientific contexts, the choice between standard and scientific notation often depends on the magnitude of the numbers involved and the need to clearly show their correct number of significant figures.
Wikipedia

Stellar Parallax

The apparent shift in position of a nearby star against the background of distant stars as observed from opposite sides of Earth's orbit around the Sun.
Stellar parallax provides the most direct and reliable method for measuring distances to nearby stars. The parallax angle is inversely related to distance - closer stars show larger parallax shifts. A star with a parallax of one arcsecond is defined to be at a distance of one parsec (about 3.26 light-years). This technique works reliably for stars within about 100 parsecs of Earth and forms the foundation of the cosmic distance ladder. The first successful measurement of stellar parallax was achieved by Friedrich Bessel in 1838 for the star 61 Cygni, providing the first direct proof that Earth orbits the Sun.
Wikipedia

Stratopause

The boundary layer between the stratosphere and mesosphere, located approximately 50 kilometers above sea level, where atmospheric temperature reaches a maximum before beginning to decrease with altitude.
The stratopause represents a critical transition zone in Earth's atmosphere where temperature behavior changes dramatically. In the stratosphere below, temperature increases with altitude due to ozone absorption of ultraviolet radiation from the Sun. At the stratopause, this warming effect reaches its peak at around -2.5°C (27.5°F), after which temperature begins to decrease with altitude in the mesosphere above. The atmospheric pressure at the stratopause is approximately 1/1000th of sea level pressure, making it an extremely thin environment. This boundary is not exclusive to Earth but occurs on any planet with an atmosphere that absorbs solar radiation, though planets like Venus and Mars that don't absorb shortwave sunlight lack a distinct stratopause.
Wikipedia UCAR Atmospheric Layers

Stratosphere

The second layer of Earth's atmosphere, extending from about 17-50 kilometers altitude, characterized by temperatures increasing or staying the same with altitude increase. This layer contains the protective ozone layer.
The stratosphere is crucial for life on Earth because it contains the ozone layer, which absorbs harmful ultraviolet radiation from the Sun. Unlike the troposphere below, temperatures increase or stay the same with increasing altitude, due to ozone (and oxygen) absorption of high frequency UV radiation. The stratosphere extends from the tropopause (top of the troposphere) to the stratopause at about 50 km altitude. Weather balloons and some specialized aircraft can reach the stratosphere. The stratosphere is above the altitude where most weather occurs. The stratosphere plays important roles in climate regulation and atmospheric chemistry.
Wikipedia

Summer Solstice

The astronomical event when the Sun reaches its highest position in the sky, resulting in the longest day and shortest night of the year in the Northern Hemisphere.
The Summer Solstice occurs around June 20-21 each year when the Sun is directly overhead at the Tropic of Cancer (23.5° North latitude). This marks the official beginning of summer in the Northern Hemisphere and winter in the Southern Hemisphere. During the summer solstice, the North Pole is tilted toward the Sun at its maximum angle, resulting in the most daylight hours for locations north of the equator. The word "solstice" comes from Latin meaning "sun stands still," referring to the apparent pause in the Sun's northward movement in the sky.
Wikipedia

Summer Time

Another term for Daylight Saving Time, referring to the practice of advancing clocks during warmer months to extend evening daylight hours.
Summer Time is the European term for what Americans call Daylight Saving Time. The practice involves moving clocks forward by one hour during the warmer months to make better use of natural daylight in the evening. In Europe, Summer Time typically begins on the last Sunday in March and ends on the last Sunday in October. The term emphasizes the seasonal nature of this time adjustment, which aims to align waking hours with daylight hours during the period when days are longest.
Wikipedia

Supergiant Elliptical Galaxy Virgo A (M87)

A massive elliptical galaxy located in the Virgo Cluster, famous for being the first galaxy to have its supermassive black hole directly imaged by the Event Horizon Telescope.
Messier 87 (M87) is one of the most massive galaxies in the local universe, containing over one trillion stars and located about 55 million light-years from Earth. The galaxy hosts a supermassive black hole with a mass of about 6.5 billion solar masses, which became the first black hole ever directly imaged when the Event Horizon Telescope captured its shadow in 2019. M87 also features a prominent relativistic jet of plasma extending thousands of light-years from its central black hole, making it an important object for studying active galactic nuclei and black hole physics. As the dominant galaxy in the Virgo Cluster, M87 plays a crucial role in our understanding of galaxy formation and evolution.
Wikipedia

Synodic Month

The time interval between successive new moons as observed from Earth, lasting approximately 29.5 days and representing the complete cycle of lunar phases.
The synodic month is what most people think of as a "lunar month" - the time from one new moon to the next new moon. This period is longer than the sidereal month (27.3 days) because Earth moves in its orbit during the lunar cycle, requiring the Moon to travel a bit farther to align with the Sun and Earth in the same configuration. The synodic month forms the basis for many calendar systems throughout history and is fundamental to understanding tidal patterns, which follow the lunar cycle. For an observer on Earth, the idea of a month is firmly connected with the synodic month, as it represents the observable cycle of lunar phases that has guided human timekeeping for millennia.
Wikipedia
