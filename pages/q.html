Qualitative Observations

Descriptive observations that don't involve numbers, focusing on qualities and characteristics that can be observed but not measured numerically.
Qualitative observations are fundamental to understanding how science works and form one half of the observational foundation of scientific investigation. Examples include observing that a solution turns bright blue, that a material feels rough to the touch, or that a reaction produces a particular odor. While qualitative observations don't provide the numerical precision needed for reproducible science, they are essential for initial discovery and understanding of phenomena.
Wikipedia

Quantitative Observations

Observations that always involve numbers, providing numerical precision that allows science to be reproducible and reliable.
Quantitative observations form the numerical foundation of scientific measurement. Instead of saying something is "hot," we might measure its temperature as 150 degrees Celsius. This numerical precision is what allows science to be reproducible and reliable. Every quantitative observation is actually composed of three essential parts: a best guess or best estimate, a tolerance or instrument limiting error, and a unit - forming a complete scientific measurement.
Wikipedia
