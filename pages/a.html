Absorption

The process by which light energy is converted to other forms of energy when electromagnetic radiation interacts with matter, resulting in the reduction of light intensity.
When light encounters matter, it can be absorbed by atoms and molecules, causing electrons to move to higher energy levels or causing molecular vibrations and rotations. The absorbed energy is typically converted to thermal energy (heat), though it can also drive chemical reactions or be re-emitted at different wavelengths. Different materials absorb different wavelengths selectively, which is why objects appear colored - they absorb certain wavelengths while reflecting others. Absorption is fundamental to many processes including photosynthesis, solar energy conversion, and the greenhouse effect. The amount of absorption depends on the material's properties, the wavelength of light, and the thickness of the material, following the Beer-Lambert law in many cases.

Accuracy

The degree of closeness between a measurement and its true or accepted value, indicating how correct a measurement is.
Accuracy is often confused with precision, but they represent different aspects of measurement quality. While accuracy measures how close you are to the true value, precision measures how close repeated measurements are to each other. A measurement can be precise but not accurate (consistently wrong), accurate but not precise (correct on average but scattered), or both accurate and precise (the ideal case). For example, if the true length of an object is 10.0 cm, measurements of 9.9, 10.0, and 10.1 cm would be accurate, while measurements of 8.1, 8.2, and 8.1 cm would be precise but not accurate.

Airglow

A faint emission of light by Earth's atmosphere, caused by various chemical reactions and energy processes occurring in the upper atmosphere, particularly in the thermosphere and mesosphere.
Airglow is a continuous, weak luminescence of the sky that occurs both day and night, though it's most visible during nighttime observations. It results from several processes including chemiluminescence (chemical reactions producing light), fluorescence (absorption and re-emission of solar radiation), and recombination of atoms and molecules that were dissociated by solar ultraviolet radiation during the day. The phenomenon creates different colored emissions at various altitudes - green oxygen emissions around 100 km, red oxygen emissions around 300 km, and sodium emissions around 90 km. Airglow can interfere with astronomical observations but also provides valuable information about atmospheric composition and dynamics.
Wikipedia

Albedo

The fraction of solar radiation reflected by a surface, expressed as a ratio between 0 (perfect absorption) and 1 (perfect reflection), or as a percentage.
Albedo is a crucial factor in Earth's energy balance and climate system. Fresh snow has a high albedo (0.8-0.9), reflecting most incoming solar radiation, while dark ocean water has a low albedo (0.06), absorbing most solar energy. This creates important feedback loops in climate change - as ice melts due to warming, it exposes darker surfaces that absorb more heat, leading to further warming. Different surfaces have characteristic albedos: forests (0.15), grasslands (0.25), deserts (0.4), and clouds (0.5-0.9). Understanding albedo is essential for climate modeling and studying planetary energy budgets.
Wikipedia

Ampere's Law

A fundamental law of electromagnetism that relates the magnetic field around a closed loop to the electric current passing through the loop, forming one of Maxwell's four equations.
Ampere's Law states that the magnetic field around a current-carrying conductor is proportional to the current and inversely related to the distance from the conductor. This law is essential for understanding how electric currents create magnetic fields and how changing magnetic fields can induce electric fields. In Maxwell's formulation, Ampere's Law was modified to include the displacement current term, which was crucial for predicting the existence of electromagnetic waves. The law explains how oscillating electric fields create oscillating magnetic fields, which in turn create oscillating electric fields, allowing electromagnetic waves (light) to propagate through space as self-sustaining disturbances. This principle is fundamental to understanding radio transmission, light propagation, and all electromagnetic phenomena.

Anacoustic Zone

A region in the upper atmosphere where sound waves cannot propagate effectively due to extremely low air density, typically beginning around 160 kilometers altitude.
The anacoustic zone exists because sound requires a medium to travel through, and at altitude of about 160 km, the atmosphere becomes so thin that there are insufficient air molecules to carry sound waves effectively. This zone begins in the lower thermosphere where the mean free path of molecules becomes comparable to or larger than the wavelength of sound. While electromagnetic radiation (like radio waves) can still propagate through this region, acoustic waves cannot. This has important implications for atmospheric studies and explains why sounds from space cannot be heard on Earth without electronic amplification and conversion.
Wikipedia

Analog Devices

Measuring instruments distinguished by having a ruled surface that you line up with an edge, needle, or other marking to take measurements.
Analog devices differ from digital devices in that they use continuous scales rather than discrete digital displays. Examples include micrometer calipers, metric metal rulers, beam balance scales, and graduated cylinders. For analog devices, the tolerance or instrument limiting error is typically determined using the "one-half rule" - taking the tolerance to be half of the least count, because we can conservatively say we can "see" 2 parts in the least count.
Wikipedia

Andromeda Galaxy (M31)

The nearest major galaxy to the Milky Way, located approximately 2.5 million light-years away and containing over one trillion stars.
The Andromeda Galaxy, also known as Messier 31 or M31, is the most distant object visible to the naked eye under dark skies. It is a spiral galaxy similar to our own Milky Way but significantly larger, spanning about 220,000 light-years in diameter. Andromeda is approaching our galaxy at about 250,000 mph and is expected to collide with the Milky Way in approximately 4.5 billion years, forming a new elliptical galaxy sometimes called "Milkomeda." The galaxy contains a supermassive black hole at its center and is accompanied by several smaller satellite galaxies, including M32 and M110.
Wikipedia

Argon

A noble gas that makes up approximately 0.93% of Earth's atmosphere by volume, making it the third most abundant gas after nitrogen and oxygen.
Argon is a chemically inert noble gas that plays an important role in atmospheric science and various industrial applications. It was the first noble gas to be discovered on Earth (1894) and gets its name from the Greek word "argos" meaning "lazy" due to its chemical inactivity. In the atmosphere, argon is produced by the radioactive decay of potassium-40 in Earth's crust and has accumulated over geological time. Its concentration remains relatively constant because it doesn't react with other atmospheric components. Argon is used in welding, lighting, and as a protective atmosphere for growing silicon crystals. Its presence in the atmosphere is also used in radiometric dating techniques.
Wikipedia

Aristotle

Ancient Greek philosopher and scientist (384-322 BCE) who made foundational contributions to logic, natural philosophy, and the scientific method, famously stating that "knowledge is the product of experience."
Aristotle's principle that "knowledge is the product of experience" forms the philosophical foundation of the modern scientific method. He emphasized that we learn about the external world through data acquired through our senses, establishing the empirical approach that underlies all scientific investigation. His systematic approach to observation and logical reasoning laid the groundwork for experimental science, making him one of the most influential figures in the development of scientific thinking.
Wikipedia

Asterism

An observed pattern or group of stars in the sky that is not a constellation, often forming recognizable shapes or being part of larger constellations.
Asterisms are more general than constellations and can be any identified star pattern. They can be part of a constellation (like the Big Dipper within Ursa Major), span multiple constellations (like the Summer Triangle formed by stars from three different constellations), or be smaller patterns within constellations. Famous asterisms include the Big Dipper, Little Dipper, Orion's Belt, the Summer Triangle, and the Winter Triangle. Unlike the 88 officially recognized constellations, asterisms are informal star patterns that have been identified by various cultures throughout history.
Wikipedia

Astrolabe

An ancient astronomical instrument used for navigation and timekeeping, consisting of a flat circular disk with a rotating overlay that models the movement of celestial objects.
The astrolabe was one of the most important scientific instruments of the medieval period, used by astronomers, navigators, and astrologers from ancient Greece through the Islamic Golden Age and into Renaissance Europe. It could determine local time, the time of sunrise and sunset, the direction of Mecca for Islamic prayers, and latitude for navigation. The instrument consists of a base plate (mater) with climate-specific star maps, a rotating star chart (rete), and a rule for measurements. While largely replaced by more accurate modern instruments, astrolabes remain important for understanding the history of astronomy and navigation, and are still used today for educational purposes and by some traditional navigators.
Wikipedia

Astrobiology

The scientific study of the origin, evolution, distribution, and future of life in the universe, including life on Earth and the possibility of extraterrestrial life.
Astrobiology combines aspects of astronomy, biology, and geology to understand how life might arise and evolve in different environments. NASA's astrobiology program addresses three fundamental questions: How does life begin and evolve? Does life exist elsewhere in the Universe? What is the future of life on Earth and beyond? This interdisciplinary field emerged in the 1990s and has become central to space exploration missions, including the search for biosignatures on Mars and the study of potentially habitable exoplanets.
Wikipedia NASA Astrobiology

Astrology

A belief system that attempts to predict human affairs and terrestrial events based on the positions and movements of celestial bodies.
Traditional astrology claims there are 12 zodiacal constellations, but the Sun actually passes through 13 constellations, including Ophiuchus between Scorpius and Sagittarius. Due to the precession of Earth's axis over 26,000 years, the Sun on your birthday is not in the astrological sign traditionally assigned to that date - it's off by about a month. For example, if traditional astrology says your Sun sign is Virgo (August 23-September 22), the Sun is actually in Virgo between September 16-October 30. While astrology has historical and cultural significance, it is not considered a science as its claims cannot be tested through the scientific method.
Wikipedia

Astronomical Unit (AU)

A unit of length defined to be exactly equal to 149,597,870.7 kilometers (92,955,807.3 miles), approximately the average distance between Earth and the Sun.
The astronomical unit serves as a fundamental measurement for distances within our solar system and provides a convenient scale for expressing planetary distances. For example, Mars orbits at about 1.5 AU from the Sun, while Jupiter is approximately 5.2 AU away. The AU is also used in defining other astronomical distance units - one parsec equals about 206,265 AU. Originally conceived as the semi-major axis of Earth's orbit, the AU was redefined in 2012 by the International Astronomical Union as an exact value to provide a stable reference for astronomical measurements.
Wikipedia

Aurorae

Spectacular light displays in the upper atmosphere caused by the interaction of charged particles from the solar wind with Earth's magnetic field and atmospheric gases.
Aurorae, commonly known as the Northern Lights (Aurora Borealis) and Southern Lights (Aurora Australis), occur when solar wind particles are channeled by Earth's magnetic field toward the polar regions. These charged particles collide with atmospheric gases at altitudes of 80-300 km, exciting oxygen and nitrogen atoms that then emit light. Oxygen produces green and red (or combined, yellow) colors, while nitrogen creates blue, magenta and purple hues. The oval-shaped auroral zones typically occur around 65-70 degrees magnetic latitude, but during strong geomagnetic storms, aurorae can be visible at much lower latitudes. Similar phenomena occur on other planets with magnetic fields, including Jupiter, Saturn, and Mars.
Wikipedia

Axial Tilt

The angle between a planet's rotational axis and the perpendicular to its orbital plane, which determines the planet's seasons and climate patterns.
Earth's axial tilt of approximately 23.5 degrees is responsible for our seasonal changes as different hemispheres receive varying amounts of direct sunlight throughout the year. During summer in the Northern Hemisphere, the North Pole tilts toward the Sun, resulting in longer days and more direct sunlight. Mars has a similar axial tilt to Earth (about 25 degrees), giving it comparable seasonal patterns, though its seasons last about twice as long due to its longer orbital period. Venus has an extreme axial tilt of about 177 degrees, meaning it rotates nearly upside down, while Uranus has an axial tilt of 98 degrees, essentially rotating on its side.
