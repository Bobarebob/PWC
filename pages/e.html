Amelia Earhart

American aviation pioneer (1897-1937) who was the first woman to fly solo across the Atlantic Ocean and mysteriously disappeared while attempting to circumnavigate the globe.
Amelia Mary Earhart became an international icon for her aviation achievements and advocacy for women's rights. She set numerous aviation records, including being the first woman to fly solo across the Atlantic Ocean in 1932. Her disappearance on July 2, 1937, during an attempt to circumnavigate the globe near Howland Island in the Pacific Ocean, remains one of aviation's greatest mysteries. Earhart's legacy continues to inspire generations of aviators and serves as a symbol of courage, determination, and the pursuit of scientific and technological advancement.
Wikipedia

Earth-Moon Distance

The average distance between the centers of Earth and the Moon, approximately 239,000 miles (384,400 kilometers), regularly measured using laser ranging to reflectors placed on the lunar surface.
The Earth-Moon distance is precisely measured by shining laser beams off retroreflectors placed on the Moon by Apollo astronauts and Soviet rovers. This distance varies due to the Moon's elliptical orbit, ranging from about 225,700 miles (perigee) to 252,000 miles (apogee). The Moon is gradually moving away from Earth at about 3.8 centimeters per year due to tidal forces. When the Moon first formed about 4.5 billion years ago, it was much closer to Earth - probably only about 24,000 miles away, or about 10 times closer than today. This distance is often used as a reference for other astronomical measurements within our solar system.
Wikipedia

Electromagnetic Spectrum

The complete range of electromagnetic radiation organized by frequency and wavelength, extending from radio waves with the longest wavelengths to gamma rays with the shortest wavelengths.
The electromagnetic spectrum encompasses all forms of electromagnetic radiation, which are fundamentally the same phenomenon - oscillating electric and magnetic fields traveling at the speed of light. The spectrum is typically divided into regions: radio waves, microwaves, infrared radiation, visible light, ultraviolet radiation, X-rays, and gamma rays. Visible light occupies only a tiny portion of the spectrum, roughly 380-700 nanometers in wavelength. Each region has distinct properties and applications: radio waves for communication, microwaves for cooking and radar, infrared for thermal imaging, ultraviolet for sterilization, X-rays for medical imaging, and gamma rays for cancer treatment. The energy of electromagnetic radiation increases with frequency, making gamma rays the most energetic and radio waves the least energetic. Understanding the electromagnetic spectrum is fundamental to astronomy, communications, medicine, and many other fields.
Wikipedia NASA Electromagnetic Spectrum

Enceladus

The sixth-largest moon of Saturn, known for its subsurface ocean and active geysers that eject water vapor and organic compounds into space.
Enceladus is considered one of the most promising places in our solar system to search for life. The Cassini spacecraft discovered that the moon's south polar region has active geysers containing water, salts, and organic molecules, suggesting a warm, potentially habitable ocean beneath its icy surface. The tidal heating from Saturn keeps this ocean liquid.
Wikipedia NASA Solar System

Endosymbiosis

A biological process where one organism lives inside another, eventually leading to the evolution of complex eukaryotic cells through the incorporation of bacterial cells as organelles.
Endosymbiosis is believed to be responsible for the evolution of mitochondria and chloroplasts in eukaryotic cells, representing a crucial step in the development of complex life on Earth. This process is considered one of the unlikely events cited in the Rare Earth Hypothesis, as it required precise conditions and timing.
Wikipedia Nature Education

Equilibrioception

The ability to sense and maintain our balance relative to the force of gravity, one of the 16 or so additional senses humans have beyond the five common senses.
Equilibrioception is an example of how we learn about the external world through sensory data, as described by Aristotle's principle that "knowledge is the product of experience." This sense works in conjunction with the vestibular system in our inner ear to help us maintain balance and spatial orientation. While we commonly think of only five senses (seeing, hearing, touching, smelling, tasting), humans actually have many more senses including equilibrioception, as well as senses of itching, hunger, and relative humidity.
Wikipedia

Europa

The fourth-largest moon of Jupiter and one of the Galilean moons, believed to have a subsurface ocean beneath its icy crust that may contain twice as much water as Earth's oceans.
Europa is a prime target for astrobiology research due to its subsurface ocean, which is kept liquid by tidal heating from Jupiter's gravitational pull. Future missions like NASA's Europa Clipper aim to study this moon's potential for harboring life. The ocean likely has direct contact with the rocky seafloor, potentially creating hydrothermal vents.
Wikipedia NASA Europa Clipper

Evaporation

The process by which liquid water changes to water vapor at temperatures below the boiling point, occurring when molecules at the surface gain enough energy to escape into the atmosphere.
Evaporation is a crucial component of the water cycle and plays a fundamental role in atmospheric processes and weather patterns. Unlike boiling, which occurs throughout a liquid at a specific temperature, evaporation happens only at the surface and can occur at any temperature above freezing. The rate of evaporation depends on temperature, humidity, wind speed, and surface area. In the atmosphere, evaporation from oceans, lakes, rivers, and soil provides the water vapor that forms clouds and precipitation. The process also has a cooling effect, which is why sweating helps regulate body temperature and why wet surfaces feel cool. Evaporation is essential for the global energy balance, as it transfers latent heat from the surface to the atmosphere.
Wikipedia

Event Horizon Telescope (EHT)

A global network of radio telescopes working together to create a virtual Earth-sized telescope capable of imaging the event horizons of supermassive black holes.
The EHT achieved the first direct image of a black hole's event horizon in 2019, capturing the supermassive black hole in galaxy M87. This groundbreaking achievement required precise coordination of telescopes around the world, using a technique called very long baseline interferometry (VLBI). The project later captured the first image of Sagittarius A*, the supermassive black hole at the center of our own Milky Way galaxy. These images provided the first direct visual evidence of black holes and confirmed predictions of Einstein's general theory of relativity.
Wikipedia Event Horizon Telescope

Exobiology

The branch of biology that deals with the search for and study of extraterrestrial life forms, now more commonly referred to as astrobiology.
The term was coined by Joshua Lederberg in the 1960s and encompasses the study of life's potential beyond Earth. Exobiology investigates how life might arise and evolve in different planetary environments and what signatures might indicate its presence. It combines astronomy, biology, chemistry, and geology.
Wikipedia NASA Exobiology

Experiment

A logical procedure that attempts to prove or disprove a hypothesis using qualitative and quantitative observations, and must be repeatable.
Experiments are the foundation of the scientific method and must be designed to test specific hypotheses through controlled conditions. A well-designed experiment isolates variables to determine cause-and-effect relationships. The results must be reproducible by other scientists to be considered valid. Experiments can be observational (like astronomical observations) or controlled laboratory studies, but all must follow rigorous protocols to ensure reliable results.
Wikipedia

Extremophiles

Organisms that thrive in extreme environmental conditions that would be lethal to most life forms, such as high temperature, acidity, radiation, or pressure.
The study of extremophiles has expanded our understanding of the limits of life and the potential for life to exist in harsh environments on other planets. Examples include organisms found near deep-sea hydrothermal vents, in highly acidic environments, and in extremely cold or radioactive conditions. They suggest life could exist in previously unimaginable environments.
Wikipedia NASA Astrobiology
