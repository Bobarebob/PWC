Tardigrades

Microscopic animals, also known as water bears, that are renowned for their ability to survive extreme conditions including radiation, dehydration, and the vacuum of space.
Tardigrades are considered extremophiles and have been used in astrobiology research to understand the limits of life's survival. Their remarkable resilience has implications for the possibility of life surviving interplanetary travel and existing in harsh extraterrestrial environments. They can enter a state called cryptobiosis, essentially suspending all biological processes until conditions improve.
Wikipedia

Technosignatures

Observable manifestations of technology that could indicate the presence of an advanced extraterrestrial civilization, such as artificial radio signals, atmospheric pollution, or megastructures.
Technosignatures represent a modern approach to SETI that goes beyond radio signals to include any detectable signs of technology. Examples might include industrial pollutants in exoplanet atmospheres, artificial lighting on the night side of planets, or large-scale engineering projects like Dyson spheres. This broader approach increases the chances of detecting advanced civilizations.
Wikipedia SETI Institute

Temperature

A measure of the average kinetic energy of particles in a substance. In astronomy, we associate temperature with anything that is incandescent or glowing.
Temperature is fundamental to understanding stellar physics and cosmology. Hotter glowing objects emit light with higher dominant frequencies and appear bluer, while cooler objects appear redder. This relationship is described by Wien's Displacement Law, which states that the dominant wavelength of a blackbody is inversely proportional to its absolute temperature. In astronomy, stars of the highest surface temperature glow blue, while stars of the lowest surface temperature glow red or brown.
Wikipedia

Temperature of the Universe

The current average temperature of the Universe is 2.73° Kelvin, as measured from the cosmic microwave background radiation.
This temperature was originally 3000° Kelvin when the cosmic microwave background was released about 380,000 years after the Big Bang, but has cooled due to the expansion of the Universe. The CMB light was initially in the visible portion of the spectrum (yellowish orange) but has been redshifted to microwave frequencies as the Universe expanded and the light wavelengths stretched. This cooling will continue as the Universe expands, eventually leading to the Big Chill scenario.
Wikipedia

Tolerance or Instrument Limiting Error

The second numerical part of a scientific measurement, representing the uncertainty or precision limit of the measuring device, often abbreviated as ILE.
In a scientific measurement like 12.34 ± 0.02 cm, the tolerance (± 0.02) indicates the precision limit of the measurement. The tolerance is chosen based on the least count of the measuring device - for digital devices, it's typically 100% of the least count, while for analog devices, it's usually 50% of the least count (the one-half rule). The tolerance and best guess should have the same number of digits to the right of the decimal point, reflecting consistent precision levels.
Wikipedia

Transit of Venus

A rare astronomical event where Venus passes directly between Earth and the Sun, appearing as a small black dot crossing the Sun's face, occurring in pairs separated by about 8 years with over a century between pairs.
Transits of Venus were historically crucial for determining the scale of the solar system by measuring the astronomical unit. The most recent transits occurred in 2004 and 2012, with the next pair not occurring until 2117 and 2125. These events provided early astronomers with a method to calculate the distance to the Sun using parallax measurements from different locations on Earth. The transit method is now used extensively to detect exoplanets, as planets orbiting other stars cause similar but much smaller dimming effects when they pass in front of their host stars.
Wikipedia

Trivial Systematic Error

A systematic error that can be easily identified and corrected through simple calibration or observational procedures, such as zeroing an instrument (if possible) or looking at an analog instrument straight on (to avoid parallax or eyeball error).
Trivial systematic errors are the most common and manageable type of systematic error in scientific measurements. Examples include zero calibration error (when an instrument doesn't read zero when it should), or parallax (eyeball) error (when you look at an analog measuring device off to one side, instead of straight on). These errors can typically be corrected by using proper measurement technique. Unlike non-trivial systematic errors, trivial systematic errors are predictable and easily remedied. For instance, if a digital caliper consistently reads 0.02 mm when its jaws are closed, this trivial zero error can be corrected by pressing the device's tare button, when its jaws are closed.
Wikipedia

Type 1a Supernova

A specific type of stellar explosion that occurs when a white dwarf star accumulates matter from a companion star and reaches a critical mass, causing a thermonuclear explosion with remarkably consistent peak brightness.
Type Ia supernovae are crucial "standard candles" for measuring cosmic distances because they all reach approximately the same peak luminosity. By comparing their apparent brightness to their known intrinsic brightness, astronomers can calculate their distance. These supernovae were the key tool used to discover the accelerating expansion of the universe and the existence of dark energy, revolutionizing our understanding of cosmology.
Wikipedia
