Tardigrades

Microscopic animals, also known as water bears, that are renowned for their ability to survive extreme conditions including radiation, dehydration, and the vacuum of space.
Tardigrades are considered extremophiles and have been used in astrobiology research to understand the limits of life's survival. Their remarkable resilience has implications for the possibility of life surviving interplanetary travel and existing in harsh extraterrestrial environments. They can enter a state called cryptobiosis, essentially suspending all biological processes until conditions improve.
Wikipedia

Technosignatures

Observable manifestations of technology that could indicate the presence of an advanced extraterrestrial civilization, such as artificial radio signals, atmospheric pollution, or megastructures.
Technosignatures represent a modern approach to SETI that goes beyond radio signals to include any detectable signs of technology. Examples might include industrial pollutants in exoplanet atmospheres, artificial lighting on the night side of planets, or large-scale engineering projects like Dyson spheres. This broader approach increases the chances of detecting advanced civilizations.
Wikipedia SETI Institute

Temperature

A measure of the average kinetic energy of particles in a substance. In astronomy, we associate temperature with anything that is incandescent or glowing.
Temperature is fundamental to understanding stellar physics and cosmology. Hotter glowing objects emit light with higher dominant frequencies and appear bluer, while cooler objects appear redder. This relationship is described by Wien's Displacement Law, which states that the dominant wavelength of a blackbody is inversely proportional to its absolute temperature. In astronomy, stars of the highest surface temperature glow blue, while stars of the lowest surface temperature glow red or brown.
Wikipedia

Temperature of the Universe

The current average temperature of the Universe is 2.73° Kelvin, as measured from the cosmic microwave background radiation.
This temperature was originally 3000° Kelvin when the cosmic microwave background was released about 380,000 years after the Big Bang, but has cooled due to the expansion of the Universe. The CMB light was initially in the visible portion of the spectrum (yellowish orange) but has been redshifted to microwave frequencies as the Universe expanded and the light wavelengths stretched. This cooling will continue as the Universe expands, eventually leading to the Big Chill scenario.
Wikipedia

Temporal Coherence (Light)

The measure of how well-correlated light waves are over time at a single point in space, determining how long the wave maintains a predictable phase relationship.
Temporal coherence describes the correlation between light waves at the same location but at different times. Light with high temporal coherence, such as laser light, maintains consistent phase relationships over long time periods, allowing it to produce stable interference patterns and have a very narrow spectral linewidth. Light with low temporal coherence, such as white light from the Sun, has rapidly changing phase relationships and a broad spectrum. Temporal coherence is quantified by the coherence time and coherence length - the distance light travels during the coherence time. Longer coherence lengths enable applications like interferometry, holography, and precision measurements. The temporal coherence of light is inversely related to its spectral bandwidth - narrower spectral lines correspond to longer coherence times. Understanding temporal coherence is crucial for laser design, optical communications, and precision optical measurements where phase stability is important.
Wikipedia

Theia

A hypothetical Mars-sized planetesimal that is believed to have collided with the early Earth about 4.5 billion years ago, leading to the formation of the Moon.
The Giant Impact Hypothesis proposes that Theia struck the early Earth with a glancing blow, vaporizing both Theia and a chunk of Earth. The debris from this collision formed a ring around Earth that eventually coalesced into the Moon. This theory explains many characteristics of the Earth-Moon system, including the Moon's relatively large size compared to Earth, the similar composition of Earth and lunar rocks, and the high angular momentum of the Earth-Moon system. The collision would have been catastrophic, likely melting the entire surface of Earth and ejecting massive amounts of material into space. Evidence for this theory comes from the isotopic similarities between Earth and Moon rocks, as well as computer simulations of planetary formation.
Wikipedia

Thermopause

The boundary between the thermosphere and exosphere, located at an altitude between 500 and 1,000 kilometers above Earth's surface, marking the transition from the atmosphere to outer space.
The thermopause represents the uppermost boundary of Earth's atmosphere, where the extremely thin air finally gives way to the vacuum of space. At this altitude, atmospheric density is so low that gas particles rarely collide with each other, and the concept of temperature becomes less meaningful in the traditional sense. The exact height of the thermopause varies significantly with solar activity - during periods of high solar activity, increased heating causes the thermosphere to expand, pushing the thermopause to higher altitudes. This variation has practical implications for satellite operations, as the changing atmospheric density affects orbital decay rates. The thermopause is also referred to as the exobase, as it marks the base of the exosphere where atmospheric escape to space becomes significant.
Wikipedia UCAR Thermosphere

Thermosphere

The fourth layer of Earth's atmosphere, extending from about 85 to 600 kilometers altitude, characterized by very high temperatures due to absorption of solar X-ray and ultraviolet radiation.
The thermosphere is where temperatures can reach 1,500°C (2,700°F) or higher, though the air is so thin that it would not feel hot to a human. This layer contains the ionosphere, where solar radiation ionizes gas molecules, creating charged particles that enable radio communication and cause the aurora. The International Space Station orbits in the lower thermosphere, where it experiences atmospheric drag that gradually lowers its orbit. The thermosphere expands and contracts significantly with solar activity - during solar maximum, increased solar radiation heats this layer and causes it to expand, increasing atmospheric drag on satellites. Despite the high temperatures, the extremely low density means there are very few molecules to transfer heat, so spacecraft in this region require thermal protection from solar radiation rather than atmospheric heating.
Wikipedia

Thunder

The acoustic shock wave resulting from the extreme heat generated by a lightning flash, creating the characteristic rumbling or cracking sound heard during thunderstorms.
Thunder is produced when lightning heats the surrounding air to temperatures as high as 54,000°F (30,000°C), causing the air to expand explosively fast and create a shock wave that becomes a booming sound wave. The sound of thunder travels at approximately 1,100 feet per second (340 meters per second), which is much slower than the speed of light, explaining why we see lightning before hearing thunder. The time delay between lightning and thunder can be used to estimate distance - dividing the seconds between flash and sound by 5 gives the approximate distance in miles. The character of thunder sounds depends on distance, with nearby lightning producing sharp cracks and distant lightning creating prolonged rumbles due to atmospheric effects and sound wave interactions.
Wikipedia NOAA Lightning Science

Tidal Locking

A gravitational phenomenon where an orbiting body always shows the same face to the object it orbits, occurring when the orbital period equals the rotational period.
The Moon is tidally locked to Earth, which is why we always see the same side (the near side) from Earth. This synchronization developed over billions of years due to tidal forces between Earth and the Moon. The Moon's rotation period (sidereal day) and its orbital period around Earth (sidereal month) are both exactly 27.25 days. Tidal locking is common in the solar system - most large moons are tidally locked to their planets, and many binary star systems are tidally locked to each other. The process involves the gradual transfer of rotational energy through tidal friction, eventually resulting in synchronous rotation.
Wikipedia

Tolerance or Instrument Limiting Error

The second numerical part of a scientific measurement, representing the uncertainty or precision limit of the measuring device, often abbreviated as ILE.
In a scientific measurement like 12.34 ± 0.02 cm, the tolerance (± 0.02) indicates the precision limit of the measurement. The tolerance is chosen based on the least count of the measuring device - for digital devices, it's typically 100% of the least count, while for analog devices, it's usually 50% of the least count (the one-half rule). The tolerance and best guess should have the same number of digits to the right of the decimal point, reflecting consistent precision levels.
Wikipedia

Total Internal Reflection

The complete reflection of light at the boundary between two transparent media when light travels from a denser to a less dense medium at an angle greater than the critical angle.
Total internal reflection occurs when light traveling through a material with a higher refractive index (like glass or water) hits the boundary with a material of lower refractive index (like air) at an angle greater than the critical angle. At this critical angle, the refracted ray would travel along the boundary, and beyond it, no light is transmitted - all light is reflected back into the denser medium. This phenomenon is the fundamental principle behind fiber optic communications, where light signals are trapped inside optical fibers by total internal reflection, allowing data to travel long distances with minimal loss. It also explains why objects underwater can appear to have a mirror-like surface when viewed from below at certain angles, and why diamonds sparkle so brilliantly - their high refractive index creates many opportunities for total internal reflection. Understanding total internal reflection is crucial for designing optical waveguides, prisms, and many optical instruments.
Wikipedia Britannica Total Internal Reflection

Transit of Venus

A rare astronomical event where Venus passes directly between Earth and the Sun, appearing as a small black dot crossing the Sun's face, occurring in pairs separated by about 8 years with over a century between pairs.
Transits of Venus were historically crucial for determining the scale of the solar system by measuring the astronomical unit. The most recent transits occurred in 2004 and 2012, with the next pair not occurring until 2117 and 2125. These events provided early astronomers with a method to calculate the distance to the Sun using parallax measurements from different locations on Earth. The transit method is now used extensively to detect exoplanets, as planets orbiting other stars cause similar but much smaller dimming effects when they pass in front of their host stars.
Wikipedia

Translucence (Light)

The property of a material that allows light to pass through but scatters it significantly, permitting illumination while preventing clear visibility of objects behind the material.
Translucent materials occupy the middle ground between transparent and opaque materials. While transparent materials allow clear vision through them and opaque materials block light entirely, translucent materials permit light transmission but scatter it in various directions. This scattering occurs due to microscopic irregularities, particles, or structural variations within the material that are comparable in size to the wavelength of light. Common examples include frosted glass, wax paper, thin fabrics, and certain plastics. Translucency is useful in applications where diffused lighting is desired, such as lampshades, privacy screens, and architectural elements. The degree of translucency can be controlled by material thickness, surface texture, and internal structure. Understanding translucency is important for lighting design, privacy applications, and creating materials with specific optical properties for both functional and aesthetic purposes.
Wikipedia

Transmission (Light)

The passage of light through a material without being absorbed or scattered, allowing the light to continue propagating through the medium.
Light transmission occurs when electromagnetic radiation passes through a material and emerges on the other side. The amount of light transmitted depends on the material's properties - transparent materials like clear glass transmit most visible light, while opaque materials block transmission entirely. Transmission is often quantified as transmittance, the fraction of incident light that passes through. Materials can have selective transmission, allowing certain wavelengths to pass while blocking others, which is how colored filters work. The transmission of light through materials is governed by absorption, scattering, and reflection processes. Understanding light transmission is crucial for designing optical components like lenses, filters, and windows, as well as for applications in photography, solar energy, and display technologies. The study of transmission properties helps in developing materials for specific optical applications and understanding how light interacts with matter.
Wikipedia

Transparency (Light)

The property of a material that allows light to pass through with minimal scattering or absorption, enabling clear visibility of objects behind the material.
Transparency is a fundamental optical property that depends on how a material interacts with light at the molecular level. Truly transparent materials, like high-quality glass or clear crystals, have regular atomic structures that don't significantly scatter or absorb visible light. The degree of transparency can vary with wavelength - materials may be transparent to some colors while absorbing others. Transparency is different from translucency, where light passes through but is scattered, preventing clear image formation. Factors affecting transparency include material purity, surface quality, thickness, and internal structure. Understanding transparency is crucial for manufacturing optical components, windows, lenses, and display screens. In nature, transparency serves important functions, such as in the cornea of the eye or in the wings of certain insects. The development of transparent materials has been essential for advances in optics, electronics, and architecture.
Wikipedia

Trivial Systematic Error

A systematic error that can be easily identified and corrected through simple calibration or observational procedures, such as zeroing an instrument (if possible) or looking at an analog instrument straight on (to avoid parallax or eyeball error).
Trivial systematic errors are the most common and manageable type of systematic error in scientific measurements. Examples include zero calibration error (when an instrument doesn't read zero when it should), or parallax (eyeball) error (when you look at an analog measuring device off to one side, instead of straight on). These errors can typically be corrected by using proper measurement technique. Unlike non-trivial systematic errors, trivial systematic errors are predictable and easily remedied. For instance, if a digital caliper consistently reads 0.02 mm when its jaws are closed, this trivial zero error can be corrected by pressing the device's tare button, when its jaws are closed.
Wikipedia

Tropic of Cancer

The northernmost latitude (23.5° North) where the Sun can appear directly overhead, marking the northern boundary of the tropical zone and the location where the Sun is directly overhead during the Summer Solstice.
The Tropic of Cancer is named after the constellation Cancer, where the Sun appeared during the summer solstice when it was first defined over 2,000 years ago. Due to the precession of Earth's axis, the Sun now appears in Gemini during the summer solstice, but the name has been retained for historical reasons. This imaginary line passes through several countries including Mexico, the Bahamas, Egypt, Saudi Arabia, India, and southern China. The angle of 23.5° corresponds to Earth's axial tilt, which is responsible for our planet's seasons. During the summer solstice (around June 21), locations on the Tropic of Cancer experience the Sun directly overhead at noon, resulting in the shortest shadows of the year.
Wikipedia

Tropic of Capricorn

The southernmost latitude (23.5° South) where the Sun can appear directly overhead, marking the southern boundary of the tropical zone and the location where the Sun is directly overhead during the Winter Solstice.
The Tropic of Capricorn is named after the constellation Capricornus, where the Sun appeared during the winter solstice when it was first defined. Like the Tropic of Cancer, due to axial precession, the Sun now appears in Sagittarius during the winter solstice, but the historical name persists. This line passes through several countries in the Southern Hemisphere including Chile, Argentina, Paraguay, Brazil, Namibia, Botswana, South Africa, Madagascar, and Australia. During the winter solstice (around December 21), locations on the Tropic of Capricorn experience the Sun directly overhead at noon, marking the beginning of summer in the Southern Hemisphere while the Northern Hemisphere experiences winter.
Wikipedia

Tropopause

The atmospheric boundary that separates the troposphere from the stratosphere above, characterized by a temperature minimum where the normal decrease of temperature with altitude stops.
The tropopause acts as a "lid" on the troposphere, typically occurring at altitudes of 6-10 miles (10-16 km) depending on latitude and season. At this boundary, temperature stops decreasing with altitude and may even begin to increase slightly, marking the transition from the weather-active troposphere to the more stable stratosphere. The tropopause is higher at the equator (up to 11 miles) and lower at the poles (about 5 miles) due to differences in atmospheric heating and circulation patterns. This boundary is crucial for weather patterns as it often acts as a barrier that limits the vertical development of thunderstorms and helps define the upper limit of most weather phenomena. The tropopause altitude varies with seasons and weather systems, rising during warm periods and lowering during cold periods.
Wikipedia UCAR Atmospheric Layers

Troposphere

The lowest layer of Earth's atmosphere, extending from the surface to about 17 kilometers altitude, where virtually all Earth-based weather occurs, and where temperature generally decreases with increasing altitude.
The troposphere contains about 75% of the atmosphere's mass and nearly all of its water vapor, making it the layer where clouds, precipitation, and Earth-based weather systems develop. Temperature typically decreases with altitude at a rate of about 6.5°C per kilometer (the environmental lapse rate), though this can vary with local conditions. The top of the troposphere is called the tropopause, which acts as a boundary that limits vertical mixing with the stratosphere above. The troposphere is thickest at the equator and thinnest at the poles due to thermal expansion from solar heating. This layer is crucial for life on Earth as it contains the oxygen we breathe and provides the atmospheric pressure necessary for liquid water to exist. All commercial aviation occurs below 43,000 feet in the troposphere.
Wikipedia

Twilight

The period of illumination before sunrise and after sunset when the Sun is below the horizon but its light still illuminates the sky through atmospheric scattering.
There are three types of twilight based on the Sun's position below the horizon: civil twilight (Sun 0-6° below horizon), nautical twilight (Sun 6-12° below horizon), and astronomical twilight (Sun 12-18° below horizon). Civil twilight provides enough light for most outdoor activities without artificial lighting. Nautical twilight is when the horizon is still visible at sea for navigation. Astronomical twilight ends when the sky becomes completely dark for astronomical observations. The duration of twilight varies with latitude and season, being longest near the poles and during summer months.
Wikipedia

Type 1a Supernova

A specific type of stellar explosion that occurs when a white dwarf star accumulates matter from a companion star and reaches a critical mass, causing a thermonuclear explosion with remarkably consistent peak brightness.
Type Ia supernovae are crucial "standard candles" for measuring cosmic distances because they all reach approximately the same peak luminosity. By comparing their apparent brightness to their known intrinsic brightness, astronomers can calculate their distance. These supernovae were the key tool used to discover the accelerating expansion of the universe and the existence of dark energy, revolutionizing our understanding of cosmology.
Wikipedia
